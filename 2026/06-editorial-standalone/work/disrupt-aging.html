Disrupt Aging was AARP's attempt to push back on the cultural narrative around aging — a book and supporting digital experience built to reframe what it means to grow older in America. The work served as both editorial argument and rallying cry, drawing on research, lived experience, and a generation of voices that the rest of culture had stopped listening to.

The brief

The cultural script around aging had calcified into something narrow and limiting — tropes of decline, irrelevance, invisibility. AARP wanted a counter-argument that would land with readers who hadn't yet identified as their target audience: people in their forties and fifties watching the conversation about themselves happen without them. Disrupt Aging needed to be a book that earned its title.

Approach

My role spanned research, writing, and editing. We built the manuscript from interviews, demographic data, and original reporting on the people quietly redefining what later life looks like. The supporting digital experience extended the argument — short essays, a hashtag campaign (#DisruptAging), and tools that invited readers to share their own counter-narratives.
