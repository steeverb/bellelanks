For New York Life, the assignment was to build a body of educational content that would rank in search and earn the kind of trust insurance buyers need before they're ready to talk to an advisor. Insurance is a category where readers don't want to be sold to; they want to understand. The content had to deliver on that.

The premise

Most life-insurance content on the open web is written for the algorithm first and the reader second. That's exactly why most of it doesn't perform — Google has gotten very good at recognizing copy that exists to rank rather than to inform. Our wager was to write the genuinely useful version of every article in the category and let the SEO follow.

The content strategy

We mapped the questions real buyers ask at each stage of consideration — from "do I even need life insurance?" through specific product comparisons — and built articles that answered them at the level of detail a careful reader would expect. Every piece was edited for plain English, structured for skim, and given enough depth that a buyer's third or fourth question was already answered on the page.

  • Topic map covering [X] categories and [Y] specific reader questions
  • House style favoring concrete examples over abstractions
  • Internal linking that built topical authority around core themes
  • Quarterly content audits to refresh top-performing pieces

What worked

Long, careful articles outperformed short, keyword-dense ones — even on competitive terms — because they earned the kind of dwell time and engagement signals that compound. The pieces that ranked best were also the ones a financial advisor would have been comfortable handing to a client.
